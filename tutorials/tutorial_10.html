1. Rural vs Urban/Suburban Voters

Question:

Suppose the proportion of rural voters in a certain state who favor a particular gubernatorial candidate is $0.45$ and the proportion of suburban and urban voters favoring the candidate is $0.60$. If a sample of 200 rural voters and 300 urban and suburban voters is obtained, what is the approximate probability that at least 250 of these voters favor this candidate?

Length Rating:
Complexity Rating:
Final Answer:

The approximate probability that at least 250 of these voters favor the candidate is $0.9686$.

Full Solution:

Let $X_1$ be the number of rural voters in the sample who favor the candidate.
Let $X_2$ be the number of urban and suburban voters in the sample who favor the candidate.

We are given: Sample size of rural voters, $n_1 = 200$. Proportion of rural voters favoring the candidate, $p_1 = 0.45$. Sample size of urban and suburban voters, $n_2 = 300$. Proportion of urban and suburban voters favoring the candidate, $p_2 = 0.60$.

$X_1$ follows a binomial distribution $B(n_1, p_1)$, so $X_1 \sim B(200, 0.45)$.
$X_2$ follows a binomial distribution $B(n_2, p_2)$, so $X_2 \sim B(300, 0.60)$.

We want to find the probability that the total number of voters favoring the candidate, $X = X_1 + X_2$, is at least 250. That is, $P(X \ge 250)$.

Since $n_1$ and $n_2$ are large, we can use the Normal approximation to the Binomial distribution.

For $X_1$:

Mean: $\mu_1 = n_1 p_1 = 200 \times 0.45 = 90$.
Variance: $\sigma_1^2 = n_1 p_1 (1 - p_1) = 200 \times 0.45 \times (1 - 0.45) = 200 \times 0.45 \times 0.55 = 49.5$.
Check condition: $n_1 p_1 = 90 > 5$ and $n_1(1 - p_1) = 110 > 5$. Approximation is valid.
So, $X_1 \approx N(90, 49.5)$.

For $X_2$:

Mean: $\mu_2 = n_2 p_2 = 300 \times 0.60 = 180$.
Variance: $\sigma_2^2 = n_2 p_2 (1 - p_2) = 300 \times 0.60 \times (1 - 0.60) = 300 \times 0.60 \times 0.40 = 72$.
Check condition: $n_2 p_2 = 180 > 5$ and $n_2(1 - p_2) = 120 > 5$. Approximation is valid.
So, $X_2 \approx N(180, 72)$.

For the Sum $X = X_1 + X_2$:

$X = X_1 + X_2$. Since $X_1$ and $X_2$ are independent, the sum $X$ is also approximately normally distributed.
Mean of $X$: $\mu_X = \mu_1 + \mu_2 = 90 + 180 = 270$.
Variance of $X$: $\sigma_X^2 = \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 = 49.5 + 72 = 121.5$.
Standard deviation of $X$: $\sigma_X = \sqrt{121.5} \approx 11.023$.
So, $X \approx N(270, 121.5)$.

Probability Calculation:

We want to find $P(X \ge 250)$. We use the continuity correction by calculating $P(X \ge 249.5)$.

Standardize the value 249.5: $Z = \frac{X - \mu_X}{\sigma_X} = \frac{249.5 - 270}{\sqrt{121.5}}$
$Z = \frac{-20.5}{11.023} \approx -1.8597$.

So, $P(X \ge 249.5) = P(Z \ge -1.8597)$.

Using the standard normal cumulative distribution function $\Phi(z) = P(Z \le z)$: $P(Z \ge -1.8597) = 1 - P(Z < -1.8597) = 1 - \Phi(-1.8597)$.

Using a Z-table or calculator, $\Phi(-1.86) \approx 0.0314$. $P(X \ge 250) \approx 1 - 0.0314 = 0.9686$.

The approximate probability that at least 250 of these voters favor the candidate is 0.9686.

2. Double Exponential Distribution Estimation

Question:

Consider the double exponential distribution given by the density function $f(x) = \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda|x|}, -\infty < x < +\infty$.

Find the point estimate of $\lambda$ using
(a) Method of Moments
(b) Method of Maximum Likelihood Estimation.

Length Rating:
Complexity Rating:
Final Answer:

(a) Method of Moments (using first absolute moment): $\hat{\lambda}_{MoM} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|}$.
(b) Maximum Likelihood Estimate: $\hat{\lambda}_{MLE} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|}$.

Full Solution:
(a) Method of Moments (MoM)

The Method of Moments involves equating theoretical moments of the distribution to the corresponding sample moments.

First theoretical moment (Mean):

$E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x f(x) dx = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda |x|} dx$.

The integrand $g(x) = x \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda |x|}$ is an odd function ($g(-x) = -g(x)$). The integral of an odd function over $(-\infty, \infty)$ is 0. So, $E[X] = 0$. Equating this to the first sample moment $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n} \sum X_i$ gives $0 = \bar{X}$. This equation does not provide an estimator for $\lambda$.

Second theoretical moment:

$E[X^2] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x^2 f(x) dx = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x^2 \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda |x|} dx$.

The integrand $h(x) = x^2 \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda |x|}$ is an even function ($h(-x) = h(x)$). $E[X^2] = 2 \int_{0}^\infty x^2 \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda x} dx = \lambda \int_{0}^\infty x^2 e^{-\lambda x} dx$.

Using the Gamma integral form $\int_{0}^\infty t^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta t} dt = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)}{\beta^\alpha}$: Here $\alpha - 1 = 2 \implies \alpha = 3$, and $\beta = \lambda$. $\int_{0}^\infty x^2 e^{-\lambda x} dx = \frac{\Gamma(3)}{\lambda^3} = \frac{2!}{\lambda^3} = \frac{2}{\lambda^3}$.

So, $E[X^2] = \lambda \times \frac{2}{\lambda^3} = \frac{2}{\lambda^2}$.

The second sample moment is $M_2 = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2$. Equating theoretical and sample moments: $E[X^2] = M_2 \implies \frac{2}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2$.

Solving for $\lambda$: $\lambda^2 = \frac{2n}{\sum X_i^2} \implies \hat{\lambda}_{MoM1} = \sqrt{\frac{2n}{\sum X_i^2}}$ (since $\lambda > 0$).

First absolute moment:

Alternatively, we can use the first absolute moment:
$E[|X|] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty |x| f(x) dx = \int_{-\infty}^\infty |x| \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda |x|} dx$.

The integrand $k(x) = |x| \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda |x|}$ is an even function. $E[|X|] = 2 \int_{0}^\infty x \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda x} dx = \lambda \int_{0}^\infty x e^{-\lambda x} dx$.

Using the Gamma integral with $\alpha=2, \beta=\lambda$: $\int_{0}^\infty x e^{-\lambda x} dx = \frac{\Gamma(2)}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1!}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}$.

So, $E[|X|] = \lambda \times \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda}$.

The corresponding sample moment is $\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|$. Equating: $\frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|$.

Solving for $\lambda$: $\hat{\lambda}_{MoM2} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|}$.

Both $\hat{\lambda}_{MoM_1}$ and $\hat{\lambda}_{MoM_2}$ are valid MoM estimators. Often, the estimator derived from the lowest possible order moment is preferred. We will report the second one, $\hat{\lambda}_{MoM_2}$.

MoM estimate: $\hat{\lambda}_{MoM} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|}$. (b) Method of Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Given a random sample $x_1, x_2, ..., x_n$, the likelihood function is: $L(\lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^n f(x_i; \lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{\lambda}{2} e^{-\lambda |x_i|} = \left(\frac{\lambda}{2}\right)^n e^{-\lambda \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|}$.

The log-likelihood function is: $\ln L(\lambda) = \ln\left[\left(\frac{\lambda}{2}\right)^n e^{-\lambda \sum |x_i|}\right] = n \ln\left(\frac{\lambda}{2}\right) - \lambda \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|$
$\ln L(\lambda) = n (\ln \lambda - \ln 2) - \lambda \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|$.

To find the maximum, differentiate with respect to $\lambda$ and set the derivative to zero: $\frac{d (\ln L)}{d\lambda} = n \left(\frac{1}{\lambda}\right) - \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|$.

Set the derivative to 0: $\frac{n}{\lambda} - \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i| = 0$
$\frac{n}{\lambda} = \sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|$
$\hat{\lambda}_{MLE} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n |x_i|}$.

Check the second derivative: $\frac{d^2 (\ln L)}{d\lambda^2} = \frac{d}{d\lambda} \left(n \lambda^{-1} - \sum |x_i|\right) = -n \lambda^{-2} = -\frac{n}{\lambda^2}$. Since $n > 0$ and $\lambda > 0$, the second derivative is negative, confirming a maximum.

MLE estimate: $\hat{\lambda}_{MLE} = \frac{n}{\sum_{i=1}^n |X_i|}$.

3. Shear Strength Estimation

Question:

The shear strength of each of the ten test spot welds is determined, yielding the following data (psi): 392, 376, 401, 367, 389, 362, 409, 415, 358, 375. ($n=10$).

(a) Assuming that shear strength is normally distributed, estimate the true average shear strength and standard deviation of shear strength using the method of maximum likelihood.

(b) Again assuming a normal distribution, estimate the strength value below which 95% of all welds will have their strengths.

Length Rating:
Complexity Rating:
Final Answer:

(a) MLE estimates: $\hat{\mu}_{MLE} = 384.4$ psi, $\hat{\sigma}_{MLE} \approx 18.86$ psi.

(b) The estimated strength value below which 95% of all welds will have their strengths is approximately $415.42$ psi.

Full Solution:

The data consists of $n=10$ observations: $x_1, ..., x_{10}$.
Data: 392, 376, 401, 367, 389, 362, 409, 415, 358, 375.

(a) MLE for $\mu$ and $\sigma$

For a normal distribution $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the Maximum Likelihood Estimators (MLEs) for the mean $\mu$ and the variance $\sigma^2$ are given by:
$\hat{\mu}_{MLE} = \bar{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_i$
$\hat{\sigma}^2_{MLE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2$
The MLE for the standard deviation $\sigma$ is $\hat{\sigma}_{MLE} = \sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2_{MLE}}$.

Calculate the sample mean $\bar{x}$:

$\sum x_i = 392+376+401+367+389+362+409+415+358+375 = 3844$.
$\hat{\mu}_{MLE} = \bar{x} = \frac{3844}{10} = 384.4$ psi.

Calculate the MLE for the variance $\hat{\sigma}^2_{MLE}$:

We need $\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2$. This can be calculated as $\sum x_i^2 - n \bar{x}^2$.
$\sum x_i^2 = 392^2 + 376^2 + 401^2 + 367^2 + 389^2 + 362^2 + 409^2 + 415^2 + 358^2 + 375^2$
$= 153664 + 141376 + 160801 + 134689 + 151321 + 131044 + 167281 + 172225 + 128164 + 140625 = 1481190$.

$\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 = 1481190 - 10 \times (384.4)^2 = 1481190 - 10 \times 147763.36$
$= 1481190 - 1477633.6 = 3556.4$.

$\hat{\sigma}^2_{MLE} = \frac{3556.4}{10} = 355.64$.

The MLE for the standard deviation is:
$\hat{\sigma}_{MLE} = \sqrt{355.64} \approx 18.8584$ psi.

The MLE estimates are $\hat{\mu} = 384.4$ psi and $\hat{\sigma} \approx 18.86$ psi (rounded).

(b) Estimate the strength value below which 95% of all welds will have their strengths.

We are looking for the 95th percentile $x_{0.95}$ such that $P(X \le x_{0.95}) = 0.95$.
Assuming $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, standardize: $P\left( Z \le \frac{x_{0.95} - \mu}{\sigma} \right) = 0.95$.

From standard normal table, $z_{0.95} \approx 1.645$.

So, $x_{0.95} = \mu + z_{0.95} \sigma$.

Using MLE estimates (invariance property):
$\hat{x}_{0.95} = \hat{\mu}_{MLE} + z_{0.95} \hat{\sigma}_{MLE}$
$\hat{x}_{0.95} = 384.4 + 1.645 \times 18.8584$
$\hat{x}_{0.95} \approx 384.4 + 31.022 \approx 415.42$ psi.

The estimated strength value is approximately 415.42 psi.

4. Confidence Interval for Stray-Load Loss

Question:

A Confidence Interval is desired for the true average stray-load loss $\mu$ (watts) for a certain type of induction motor when the line current is held at 10 amps for a speed of 1500 rpm. Assume that stray-load loss $X$ is normally distributed $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma = 3.0$.

(a) Compute a 95% CI for $\mu$ when $n=25$ and $\bar{x}=58.3$.

(b) Compute a 99% CI for $\mu$ when $n=100$ and $\bar{x}=58.3$.

(c) How large must $n$ be if the width of the 99% interval for $\mu$ is to be 1.0?

Length Rating:
Complexity Rating:
Final Answer:

(a) The 95% CI is $(57.124, 59.476)$ watts.

(b) The 99% CI is $(57.5272, 59.0728)$ watts.

(c) Required sample size $n = 239$.

Full Solution:

Assume $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with known $\sigma = 3.0$.

(a) 95% CI for $\mu$ with $n=25, \bar{x}=58.3$

Formula: $\bar{x} \pm z_{\alpha/2} \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$.
For 95% CI, $\alpha = 0.05 \implies \alpha/2 = 0.025$. $z_{0.025} = 1.96$.

Margin of Error $E = 1.96 \times \frac{3.0}{\sqrt{25}} = 1.96 \times \frac{3.0}{5} = 1.176$.

CI: $58.3 \pm 1.176 \implies (57.124, 59.476)$ watts.

(b) 99% CI for $\mu$ with $n=100, \bar{x}=58.3$

For 99% CI, $\alpha = 0.01 \implies \alpha/2 = 0.005$. $z_{0.005} \approx 2.576$.

Margin of Error $E = 2.576 \times \frac{3.0}{\sqrt{100}} = 2.576 \times \frac{3.0}{10} = 0.7728$.

CI: $58.3 \pm 0.7728 \implies (57.5272, 59.0728)$ watts.

(c) Sample size $n$ for a 99% CI width of 1.0

Width $W = 2 \times E = 2 \times z_{\alpha/2} \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$.
We want $W = 1.0$. Use $z_{0.005} = 2.576$ and $\sigma=3.0$.

$1.0 = 2 \times 2.576 \times \frac{3.0}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{15.456}{\sqrt{n}}$

$\sqrt{n} = 15.456 \implies n = (15.456)^2 \approx 238.89$.

Since $n$ must be an integer, round up: $n = 239$.
